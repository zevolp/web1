The allure of 8-bit and chiptune music to composers is not necessarily always a function of nostalgia (although that certainly never hurts). To the musicians that work within the medium, there is a charm and character to the limitations of emulated chip music.

The entirety of the original Super Mario soundtrack, of the first Megaman games' level music, of even the first Castlevania installments: all of these are extraordinary compositions that abide by the strict limit of instrument channels that the NES required.

Composing emulator-ready music has always been a delicate balance of creative engagement and puzzle-solving. Without an expansive, nigh-limitless piano roll, writers use the tools at their disposal to fill the space and sound.

FamiTracker is every tool you need it to be.

Why FamiTracker?
Traditional MIDI-roll programs are rarely equipped to handle true NSF-style active tracking - their primary function is to arrange pre-recorded audio clips on a timeline. The complexity and nuances of these audio bites have grown dramatically over the years, but even advanced virtual instrument suites are not actively synthesising sounds.

This process of active synthesis is essential to the production of true, emulation-ready chiptune and 8-bit music. MIDI-roll tools are powerful, but they are fundamentally incompatible with this process. By contrast, 'tracker'-styled tools enable that live synthesizing from nearly any platform.

Historically, 8-bit music was composed on proprietary software (or, for some of the oldest examples, partially drafted out on graph paper) by game designers. For a long time, composing with those lo-fi sounds was a restricted practice: first, to devices that already had the hardware necessary to read and produce the sounds, and second, to users who were able to hijack those devices in order to compose music on them. (This hijacking is not covered in the warranty.)

Popular device choices are determined as much by their limitations as by their advantages. The Nintendo GameBoy Color and GameBoy Advanced have enjoyed exceptional popularity with composers as both conduits of emulation and for their distinctive, charming, and limited range of instrumentation.

FamiTracker exists to bridge the gap between firmware emulation and users unable to acquire the tools required for that practice. There's no need to physically break open a vintage handheld console or to solder connections on a dusty sound board - FamiTracker, like other tracker applications, operates the same way that sound emulators do.

Using FamiTracker doesn't mean you can't also circle back to the traditional hardware of chiptune and 8-bit hardware. This application has a built-in write function to generate NSF format filetypes. Chiptunes made in FamiTracker can be exported immediately in an NES-ready format.
